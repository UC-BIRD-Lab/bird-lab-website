A poster is a condensed version of a talk: the poster does the job your slides normally
would, but you still prepare and practice the short talk you'll give beside it. The lab's
[communication framework]({{ '/lab-guide/presentations/' | relative_url }}#core-principles)
applies here with the volume turned up: answer *so what?* and *who cares?*, and maximize
signal-to-noise.

In a poster session, noise is expensive. If a poster looks overwhelming, people walk right
past; if it stays overwhelming, they'll walk off mid-sentence. **Pick one storyline and
stick to it.**

Consider your audience, too. A poster intended for a room of experts should look very
different from one intended for the general public.

## Sections and layout

- Posters carry many of the same sections as a talk: introduction, methods, results, conclusion, and references. (The poster itself doesn't need an abstract, though the venue may ask for one separately.)
- Use a grid. Put each section in an aligned box, with consistent white space between boxes.
- Avoid boxes that stretch edge to edge; aim for about 11 words (~45 characters) per line, and keep boxes under ~10 lines.
- Don't justify the text; with so few words it ends up with awkward spacing.

## Title

<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
🔍 **Ask yourself:** if you saw your poster from across the room, would you stop?
</aside>

- Declarative and exciting: it has to hook someone walking by.
- Legible from across the room. **Treat 54 pt as the minimum worth considering, and go larger if the layout allows.** On one of Christina's own posters the title was arguably still too small at that size; when in doubt, err big.
- One or two lines, in sentence case. Don't use ALL CAPS; it's hard to read.

## Section headers

- Like slide titles, a bare "Introduction" isn't informative; use a declarative statement (e.g. "Introduction: *your key point*").
- Sentence case reads more easily than Title Case. Use a sans-serif font for titles and headers, and keep them all the same size and type.

## Body text

- A simple, light background color is fine and can look great.
- Keep all body text one font style and size (**28 pt is a good start**), and minimize words.
- Add emphasis with *italics*; underlining reads a bit aggressive.
- Acronyms grab the eye; if one is distracting, drop its font size slightly.
- Every word must be legible, including figure labels. You may need to relabel a figure so its text is large enough.
- Tables are more acceptable than in a talk, but still avoid them where you can, and format them cleanly.

## What goes in each section

### Introduction / background

An overview of the field and the motivating goal, one or two relevant recent advances (not
your own), and how your work fills the gap. Keep it to 1–3 sentences, and leave out anything
that isn't immediately relevant.

### Methods

How your methods answer that gap, and briefly why you chose them over the alternatives. Aim
for 3–5 sentences, plus a methods figure if you can make one.

### Results

The findings that answer the question you set up, carried mostly by figures rather than text.
Lead with a declarative header that states the result itself, not the word "Results". Let one
or two well-chosen figures do the work, and guide the eye: highlight the key takeaway on the
figure, and use a single summary or comparative figure to pull the panels into one story.
Keep the words to a minimum, ideally a short takeaway line per figure.

### Conclusion

What the results mean and how they close the gap you opened in the introduction, the broader
implication (for us, often what it suggests for engineered flight or aircraft design), and,
if useful, where the work goes next. Keep it to 1–3 sentences; this is the sentence a passer-by
should leave with.

## Figures and color

- Illustrations are a powerful, fast way to convey information; lean on them.
- Use vector graphics (`.svg`/`.pdf`); bitmaps pixelate badly when printed. A high-resolution `.png` is an okay fallback.
- Choose color consistently and intentionally, use it for emphasis, and make it accessible; color blindness is common. Color combinations (and even single colors) carry meaning and emotion, so choose on purpose.
- For figure design itself, see [Figures]({{ '/lab-guide/figures/' | relative_url }}).

## A five-minute critique exercise

Before you design anything, look at Colin Purrington's deliberately terrible ["Pigs in
Space" example poster and his poster-design guide](https://colinpurrington.com/tips/academic/posterdesign)
(credit: Colin Purrington) and list everything wrong with it. Information overload, a
decorative background, illegible text: spotting the problems there makes them much easier
to avoid on your own poster.

## Learning from real posters

A few of Christina's own posters, annotated with what worked and what she'd change. Every
poster is a draft you learn from; look for the declarative section headers, the highlighted
key takeaways, and the summary or comparative figure that carries the story, and notice the
honest notes about titles set too small or a section that carried too many words.

## Making and printing it

<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
**Billing & shipping.** Chart strings and reimbursements are covered on **Pay for things** in the [member portal]({{ '/portal/' | relative_url }}); if you'd rather ship the printed poster than carry it, see **Ship Packages**.

- You can build a poster as a single large slide in PowerPoint or Keynote, or use Google Slides, Inkscape, or Illustrator (paid).
- Include the appropriate funding-agency logos; for the lab and UC Davis logos, see [Logos & branding]({{ '/lab-guide/logos-branding/' | relative_url }}).
- Check your event's poster sizing and formatting guide *before* you start.
- Design in **CMYK** for printing. Printers convert colors to CMYK, and an RGB palette can look muted after conversion.

## Want to stand out?

Beyond the poster itself, a physical demo, a 3D-printed model, a tablet or screen, or even
VR can draw people in.

## If you need an abstract

Some venues ask for a short abstract at submission, often no more than 250 words or 600
characters, so it will be *very* short. Cover why the work matters, the problem and goal,
your methods, and your results.
