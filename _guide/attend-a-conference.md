Conferences are a great way to meet the experts in your field, learn the
state of the art, and enjoy talking about research.

## Eligibility

- **If you're first author on a lab paper that year,** the lab will typically
  fund **one** relevant conference per calendar year (subject to available funds).
- **Want to attend more than one?** Apply for travel awards or student support —
  Christina will help.
- **Before you book anything,** get PI approval for (a) the conference, (b) the
  estimated budget, and (c) what you're presenting. Without advance approval,
  reimbursement is not guaranteed.
- **Two weeks before travel,** present a practice talk or poster in lab meeting.
- **After,** share five minutes of takeaways and update the lab publications list.

## Funding

**Typically lab-funded:** registration, travel (flight / train / mileage),
lodging, reasonable meals and local transit, and poster printing.

**Not covered:** extra nights for personal travel, upgrades (business class,
premium hotels), alcohol or expensive meals, and avoidable late-booking fees.

**How payment works:** in most cases you pay upfront and get reimbursed — keep
itemized receipts. If the lab needs to pay directly (e.g., registration on the
P-card), request that during the approval step.

## Choosing a conference

Pick conferences that maximize **audience fit**, **feedback quality**, and
**career value**. It's valuable to alternate between engineering- and
biology-focused conferences over time, but choose what best serves your project
and goals this year.

## The workflow

1. **Plan and get approval** *(before submitting or booking).* Send Christina the
   conference name, dates, and location; what you're submitting (poster / talk /
   paper); a rough budget; and whether you'll apply for travel awards
   (recommended). **Don't book flights or hotels until you get a written "yes."**
2. **Submit an abstract.** Conference titles can be less declarative than journal
   titles, since results can evolve. The
   [Nature summary-paragraph style](https://www.nature.com/documents/nature-summary-paragraph.pdf)
   is a good default structure.
3. **Write the conference paper** (if required). Keep it tight; aim for a version
   that can grow into a journal submission after incorporating feedback.
4. **Practice your presentation** to the lab at least two weeks before you travel,
   so you can incorporate feedback first.
5. **Attend and represent the lab well.** Network intentionally and use good
   judgment in professional settings.
6. **Share back** at the next lab meeting: five minutes of top insights and one
   or two actionable ideas, and update the
   [publications]({{ '/publications/' | relative_url }}) list.
