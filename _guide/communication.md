Communication in this lab is guided by three principles: communicate early, communicate clearly, and assume good intent. Most expectations below follow from these principles.

## Expectations

### Christina's expectations from you

- **Raise questions and problems early**, rather than waiting until a deadline is close.
- **Share drafts early** if you'd like feedback. Incomplete work is far easier to
  improve than a polished draft submitted the night before.
- **Keep Christina posted** when priorities or timelines change.
- **Bring a proposed solution** when you can. It doesn't have to be the right one; thinking
  through options usually leads to a more productive discussion.
- **Reply promptly during the work day.** Christina answers email quickly and
  appreciates the same, though she knows people work differently, so reply when you
  can. If you need more time, a brief acknowledgment is always welcome.
- **Protect off-hours.** Avoid non-urgent email or Slack outside the work day or on
  weekends unless agreed in advance.
- **Loop in Christina on external contacts.** Discuss in advance and cc her the first time you contact someone outside the lab on its behalf.
- **Share your communication needs.** If something here doesn't fit yours, tell Christina and we can look at compromises.

<div class="callout callout--warn" markdown="1">
**Give co-authors time to review.** Christina and your co-authors need enough lead
time to give a manuscript or abstract meaningful feedback. If work arrives too late
for that, Christina reserves the right to hold it back from submission, or to
withdraw conference attendance. This protects the quality of the work and everyone
whose name is on it. When you give reasonable notice, she commits to doing the same
in return.
</div>

### What you can expect from Christina

<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
Christina often **schedules messages** to send during work hours, so a late-night
send time doesn't mean she expects a late-night reply.
</aside>

- **Weekly meetings.** Graduate researchers have a weekly one-on-one in addition to lab meetings.
- **Prompt replies.** Christina typically acknowledges your email within one to two days.
- **Timely, specific feedback.** She responds substantively when you share work with reasonable lead time.
- **Respect for your off-hours.** She protects your time and models sustainable boundaries.
- **Transparency about funding and plans.** You should never feel unsure about your funding; ask as needed.
- **A growth mindset.** Running a lab and mentoring are a learning process; Christina does not consider herself a master of it and stays open to improving.
- **Support and guidance.** Technical and non-technical alike; everyone is welcome to bring issues to Christina.

### How to engage with peers

- **Ask others how they prefer to communicate.** We tend to use Slack for direct messages and topic-specific discussions.
- **Be direct and kind.** No one can read minds. If you need something, say so respectfully.
- **Assume good intent.** You may not know what someone else is dealing with, so approach disagreements with curiosity before judgment.
- **Keep official communication professional.** Write messages you would be comfortable having forwarded to collaborators, funders, or future colleagues.
- **Resolve conflict among yourselves first.** Loop in Christina if further
  mediation is needed.
- **Undergraduates should be supported by senior members.** Academia can be intimidating at first. Please support our early-career members; see [Mentoring]({{ '/lab-guide/mentoring/' | relative_url }}).

## Picking the right channel

| You need | Use | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| A quick or urgent reply | Slack | Best for short, time-sensitive questions. Keep each thread to one topic so it stays easy to follow. |
| A longer or detailed reply | Email | Use a numbered list when you're asking for several things, and flag any deadline with its date and time. Christina acknowledges within one to two days with an action plan. |
| A meeting | [when2meet](https://www.when2meet.com) | Send a few time options and say whether you need 30 or 60 minutes. |
| To share documents | Email | Send documents over email so there is a clear written record, rather than only over Slack. |

## Giving and receiving feedback {#giving-and-receiving-feedback}

Feedback, especially constructive feedback, can be uncomfortable, but accepting and learning from it is a vital part of good science. It is about the work, not the person, and giving and receiving it
well is a skill everyone here is expected to practice.

**Giving feedback**

- Make sure the recipient is ready for it; if unsure, ask.
- Balance warm and cool feedback: name strengths as well as areas to improve.
- Focus on the work, not the person. Be specific; avoid a vague "nice job!".
- Be timely, but when feedback is mostly critical, take time to plan how you deliver it.
- Follow up. Feedback is an ongoing process, so acknowledge the positive changes you see.

**Receiving feedback**

- Assume the feedback is offered in good faith and meant to help.
- Listen attentively and make sure you understand it, even if you disagree.
- Ask clarifying questions.
- Reflect. You're not obligated to act on all feedback. If you disagree, consider a second opinion. However, feedback related to safety, lab policy, or technical decisions that affect the quality or integrity of the research is not optional.
- Follow up to get multiple rounds where possible.

## Resolving conflict

<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
For our shared norms in group settings, see
[Discussion guidelines]({{ '/lab-guide/discussion-guidelines/' | relative_url }}).
</aside>

Tips for constructive resolution:

- **Raise issues early.** Silence can lead to escalation or resentment.
- **Define the conflict.** Is it structural, a communication breakdown, or a clash
  of expectations?
- **Manage your emotions first.** Collect your thoughts before confronting an issue.
- **Acknowledge your part.** Even when you believe you're mostly right, reflecting on your own contribution can make it easier to find common ground.
- **Listen actively.** Reflect back what you heard before responding. Aim to understand what someone meant, not just the exact words they used.
- **Ask for help.** Reach out to Christina or a neutral third party mediator if a problem is feeling overwhelming or unresolved.

<div class="callout" markdown="1">
**"I" statements.** A useful frame:
*"I feel ___ when (you) ___ because ___. What I'm hoping we might try is ___."*
Identify your feelings, their cause, the context for *why*, and the need or ideal
you'd like to work toward, adapted to your own voice and culture.
</div>
