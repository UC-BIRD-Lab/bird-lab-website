A few well-kept profiles make your work findable and make sure the right work is
attributed to the right you. The best reason to keep them is unglamorous but
solid: they disambiguate your identity across journals and institutions, and they
give search tools (increasingly AI-driven) clean, consistent signals to describe
you accurately. Claims that profiles directly boost your citations are weaker than
they sound, so treat this as low-effort infrastructure rather than a growth
strategy. Set the anchors up early, keep them current, and cross-link them.

## The two anchors

Start with these during your first weeks and use your UC Davis email while you're
here. They carry the strongest, best-evidenced benefits: identity, attribution,
and discoverability.

- **[ORCID](https://orcid.org)**: a permanent iD that uniquely identifies you and
  travels with you between institutions and email addresses. Use it on every
  submission; most publishers and funders now require it.
- **[Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com)**: your public citation record,
  and the profile most people (and tools) check first.

## Situational profiles

Useful depending on your field and goals, but lower priority than the anchors:

- **[ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net)**: for sharing full texts and
  connecting with other researchers.
- **[FigShare](https://figshare.com)**: for citable datasets, figures, and
  supplementary materials.

## Keep them current and linked

A stale or inconsistent profile is worse than none: it misattributes or hides your
work, and it gives search tools conflicting signals.

- **Use one canonical name and affiliation** everywhere, exactly as it appears on
  your papers, so your outputs group correctly.
- **Cross-link your profiles** to each other and to your UC Davis page. That
  triangulation is what lets both people and AI tools describe you accurately.
- **Add each new output** (paper, preprint, dataset) as it appears, and link your
  ORCID iD so records sync automatically where possible.
- **Keep a CV current** alongside your profiles: update it the day you have news,
  not once a year when you need it.

## If you're headed to industry

Industry hiring reads different signals than academia. A public **GitHub** with
real, documented projects is checked by most technical recruiters, and a working
portfolio or demo often carries more weight than a polished CV. **LinkedIn**
tends to be the hub that ties your profiles, resume, and projects together. If
this is your path, keep your best code public and readable (see
[Code]({{ '/lab-guide/code/' | relative_url }})) and tailor a one-to-two-page
resume per role.

## Communicate your work (optional)

Profiles make your work findable; talks and plain-language summaries make it land.
When your research reaches a wider audience (a
[talk]({{ '/lab-guide/presentations/' | relative_url }}),
[poster]({{ '/lab-guide/posters/' | relative_url }}), press piece, or public
summary), keep the framing accurate and free of hype, in line with how the lab
talks about its work, and loop in Christina before representing the lab publicly.

A public social presence is genuinely optional and the evidence for its payoff is
thin, so only invest if you enjoy it. If you do, the research community has largely
moved to [Bluesky](https://bsky.app) rather than X.
