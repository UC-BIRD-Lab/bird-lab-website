<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
Mentoring is a skill you learn by doing and by asking for feedback, exactly like
the science. Christina does not consider herself a master of it and keeps trying to
improve; you are invited to do the same.
</aside>

Almost everyone here mentors someone. Postdocs and graduate students help mentor
the undergraduates, senior students support newer ones, and Christina mentors the
whole lab. Good mentoring here means advancing each person's own goals, normalizing
failure as a routine part of research, and helping people grow into independent
scientists. This page is how we do it well. It draws on the University of Michigan
Rackham guides linked at the bottom and on how the lab already works.

## Mentoring is more than advising

An advisor guides academic progress. A mentor takes an interest in the whole
person: their goals, their growth, and their next step, which may lead somewhere
different from yours. Advance the goals *they* want, not the ones you would pick,
and set goals that respect differences in work styles, backgrounds, and obligations.

## Mentoring is a team effort

<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
🔍 **Ask yourself:** does the person I mentor have more than one person they can go
to, or am I their only source of support?
</aside>

No single mentor can meet every need. We mentor in layers: an undergraduate has a
day-to-day mentor (a grad student or postdoc), who has Christina, and everyone
benefits from peers. Help the person you mentor build a small team, and introduce
them to others when a question is outside what you can offer.

## Start with shared expectations

<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
The lab backs mentoring with weekly [one-on-ones and lab meetings]({{ '/lab-guide/meetings/' | relative_url }}),
quarterly [goals]({{ '/lab-guide/goal-setting/' | relative_url }}) and annual reviews,
[conference]({{ '/lab-guide/conferences/' | relative_url }}) support, and quarterly
socials and research retreats.
</aside>

The strongest mentoring relationships agree on expectations early, before
friction sets in. Onboarding already starts this: a step of the start guide is to
"Consider your goals and mentoring needs," and the first meeting with Christina
uses those answers to set goals. In your first few meetings together, talk through:

- **Meeting cadence.** How often you'll meet, and how to reach you between meetings.
- **Communication.** Preferred channels and response times; see [Communication]({{ '/lab-guide/communication/' | relative_url }}).
- **Goals and mentoring needs.** Their long-term direction and what they need from mentoring. This shapes an Individual Development Plan and this quarter's plan; see [Goal setting]({{ '/lab-guide/goal-setting/' | relative_url }}).
- **Feedback.** How and when you'll give it, and what a first draft should look like before it reaches you.
- **Authorship and credit.** Set expectations up front; see [Authorship policy]({{ '/lab-guide/authorship-policy/' | relative_url }}).

The Individual Development Plan is reviewed and updated annually, and quarterly
SMART goals are used to assess progress and adjust mentoring needs as people grow.

## Give feedback that helps

Be timely, concrete, and constructive. Balance warm and cool: name what worked as
well as what to change, and focus on the work, not the person. Hold people to high
standards *and* make clear you believe they can meet them. Our full norms for
[giving and receiving feedback]({{ '/lab-guide/communication/' | relative_url }}#giving-and-receiving-feedback)
apply to mentoring too.

## Support the whole person

<div class="callout" markdown="1">
If someone falls behind, don't assume it means they don't care. They may be
exhausted, stuck, or unsure what to do next. Ask, and keep in regular contact,
especially with anyone who goes quiet.
</div>

Many new researchers quietly wonder whether they belong. Reassure them, and
remember that a bird's-eye "you're doing fine" from a mentor carries real weight.
Watch for signs someone is struggling. You are not expected to diagnose or treat
anything; you are expected to notice, to be kind, and to point them toward
[support resources]({{ '/lab-guide/support-resources/' | relative_url }}).

<div class="callout" markdown="1">
**Normalize failure.** Encourage people to share challenges as openly as successes.
Talking about what went wrong helps destigmatize failure, a necessary practice in
research, and it often surfaces better ideas. Help them recognize their wins too,
and look back at how far they've come since joining. This is a core aim of
mentoring here, not a side note. The same openness is expected in
[lab meetings]({{ '/lab-guide/meetings/' | relative_url }}).
</div>

## Model what you expect

The clearest thing you teach is how you behave. Handle data honestly (see
[Data management]({{ '/lab-guide/data-management/' | relative_url }})), credit
work fairly, cite carefully, and admit your own mistakes. People remember how they
were treated far longer than any single piece of advice.

## Mentor across difference

You can be an excellent mentor to someone whose background differs from yours. What
you share, a commitment to the work and a desire to see them succeed, matters more
than what differs. Discuss differences openly rather than ignoring them, and no one
should be asked to speak for a whole group. See our
[discussion guidelines]({{ '/lab-guide/discussion-guidelines/' | relative_url }}).

## Know your limits

You don't have to offer everything. Be clear about what you can and can't give, and
refer people to another mentor, to Christina, or to campus resources when that
serves them better. Knowing when to hand off is part of mentoring well.
