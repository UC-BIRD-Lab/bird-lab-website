Conferences are a great way to meet the experts in your field, learn the
state of the art, and enjoy talking about research.

## Eligibility

- **If you're first author on a lab paper that year,** the lab will typically
  fund **one** relevant conference per calendar year (subject to available funds).
- **Want to attend more than one?** Apply for [travel awards]({{ '/lab-guide/funding-applications/' | relative_url }})
  or student support, and Christina will help.

## Funding

**Typically lab-funded:** registration, travel (flight/train/mileage),
lodging, reasonable meals and local transit, and poster printing.

**Not covered:** extra nights for personal travel, upgrades (business class,
premium hotels), alcohol or expensive meals, and avoidable late-booking fees.

<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
See [Travel]({{ '/lab-guide/travel/' | relative_url }}) for the full booking and reimbursement policy.
</aside>

**How payment works:** in most cases you pay upfront and get reimbursed, so keep
itemized receipts. If the lab needs to pay directly (e.g., registration on the
P-card), request that during the approval step.

## Choosing a conference

Pick conferences that maximize **audience fit**, **feedback quality**, and
**career value**. It's valuable to alternate between engineering- and
biology-focused conferences over time, but choose what best serves your project
and goals this year.

## The workflow

<aside class="marginnote" markdown="1">
**Logging time away.** Record conference days in the Vacation Tracker with the conference name as the reason. They don't count against your vacation allowance unless you extend for personal time. See [Time off & vacation]({{ '/lab-guide/time-off-vacation/' | relative_url }}) for how to log it.
</aside>

1. **Plan and get approval** *(before submitting or booking).* Send Christina the conference name, dates, and location; what you're submitting (poster/talk/paper); a rough budget; and whether you'll apply for travel awards (recommended). **Don't book flights or hotels until you get a written "yes."**
2. **Submit an abstract.** Conference titles can be less declarative than journal titles, since results can evolve. The [Nature summary-paragraph style](https://www.nature.com/documents/nature-summary-paragraph.pdf) is a good default structure.
3. **Write the conference paper** (if required). Keep it tight; aim for a version that can grow into a journal submission after incorporating feedback.
4. **Practice your [talk]({{ '/lab-guide/presentations/' | relative_url }}) or
   [poster]({{ '/lab-guide/posters/' | relative_url }})** to the lab at least two weeks before you travel, so you can incorporate feedback first.
5. **Attend and represent the lab well.** Network intentionally and use good
   judgment in professional settings.
6. **Share back** at the next lab meeting: five minutes of top insights and one or two actionable ideas, and update the
   [publications]({{ '/publications/' | relative_url }}) list.
