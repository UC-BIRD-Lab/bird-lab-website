All talks should be curated and adjusted to your current audience — undergraduates,
graduate students, hiring managers, and so on. Ask who your audience is before you
design the talk. (With thanks to [Jean-Luc Dumont](https://www.principiae.be/X0101.php)
and Doug Altshuler, whose emphasis on clear communication inspires this page.)

## Consider your audience

What is their level of expertise? How formal is the talk? What technical depth are
they comfortable with? What questions will they have? Is the goal to educate or to
persuade?

## Determine your key takeaways

Every audience has two questions: *so what?* and *who cares?* Answer them as
succinctly as possible. Your audience will remember only a few salient points — the
[Rule of Three](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_three_(writing)) is a good
default, but don't invent a third point to round things out. **Simpler is always
better.**

<div class="callout callout--warn" markdown="1">
Avoid presenting your research like a detective novel. Your audience should know
immediately why they should care, or you lose their attention.
</div>

## Graphics, data, and color

- Make all graphics and data legible to every audience member.
- Use color schemes consistently and intentionally, and ensure they're accessible to a colorblind audience ([accessible palette generator](https://venngage.com/tools/accessible-color-palette-generator)).
- **Always** use vector graphics (`.svg` or `.pdf`); high-quality `.png` if you must.
- Keep graphics directly relevant; build up complex diagrams with transitions; avoid laser pointers.
- Be deliberate with data: avoid tables where possible, label all axes and keys, and build up data-heavy graphs in parts. Watch the *lie factor* and *data-to-ink ratio*.

## Craft your slides

A typical talk includes a title slide, introduction/background, a roadmap/outline,
methods, results, conclusions, future directions, and acknowledgements.

**Do:** use a legible font at a large size; keep backgrounds simple (plain white
preferred); keep a consistent format; use color or weight to highlight takeaways;
use vector graphics. **Avoid:** irrelevant images, too many bullet points, full
sentences in bullets, and distracting color schemes.

- **Title slide:** title plus all authors and affiliations; a simple eye-catching image or video and any logos. Treat it as a placeholder — don't talk to it.
- **Roadmap:** always include one and refer back to it throughout; use creative graphics, not a list-based outline.
- **Body slides:** simple and to the point; include page numbers; build up bullets as you speak; use "appear" or "fade" transitions.
- **Transition slides:** large fonts and contrasting colors to distinguish sections.
- **Conclusion:** acknowledge contributors and funding (check with Christina), highlight key takeaways, and outline future directions. End on a confident note so the audience knows when to applaud.
