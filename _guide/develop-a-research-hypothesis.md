A research hypothesis is a **concise, testable statement** about how a system
behaves or why a phenomenon occurs. It should propose an explanation (not just a
description), lead to specific observable predictions, and be possible to confirm
or reject with attainable data.

## What makes a good hypothesis

A strong hypothesis in the BIRD Lab is:

1. **Grounded in prior work** — based on literature, theory, or existing data, so you can point to *why* the expectation is reasonable.
2. **Expectation → predictions** — it states what you expect (a relationship, pattern, or mechanism), and that expectation implies measurable predictions.
3. **Testable and falsifiable** — some conceivable data would count *against* it, and you can distinguish it from competing hypotheses.

## Common hypothesis forms

**Relationship-based:** *If X changes, Y will change in [direction], because [reason].*
e.g. "If gust intensity increases, peak roll rate during recovery will increase
because larger corrective torques are required."

**Pattern-based:** *[Behavior/field] will show [specific pattern] under [conditions], consistent with [reason].*
e.g. "In formation flight, follower positions will cluster near the leader's
upwash region."

**Mechanism-based:** *[Phenomenon] occurs because [mechanism], leading to [observable consequence].*
e.g. "Rapid yaw turns are generated primarily by asymmetric wingbeat kinematics,
leading to characteristic asymmetries in wingtip paths."

<div class="callout callout--warn" markdown="1">
**Things that are *not* hypotheses:** goals ("we want to understand…"), methods
("we will use high-speed cameras…"), descriptive plans ("we will measure wingbeat
frequency…"), vague/unfalsifiable claims ("birds use complex strategies…"), bare
predictions with no context ("wingbeat frequency will increase"), tautologies, and
design wishes ("our controller should be robust"). When in doubt, ask: *does this
make a specific, testable claim about how the system behaves under identifiable
conditions, in a way that could turn out to be wrong?*
</div>

## From question to hypotheses

1. **Define the phenomenon** — what exactly are you trying to explain or predict?
2. **Summarize what's known** — a short paragraph or bullet list from the literature/prior data.
3. **List plausible explanations** — morphology, aerodynamics, control, sensing, environment, task demands.
4. **Write competing hypotheses** — H₀ (null), then H₁, H₂ as alternative mechanisms or relationships.
5. **Derive discriminating predictions** — for each, "if this is true, we should see ___," focusing on predictions that *differ* across hypotheses.
6. **Check feasibility** — can you actually measure what you need with available tools and time?

## Hypothesis checklist

- [ ] Grounded in literature, data, or theory
- [ ] States a clear expectation (relationship, pattern, or mechanism)
- [ ] Implies specific, measurable predictions
- [ ] Is falsifiable (you can say what would contradict it)
- [ ] Has at least one competing hypothesis (plus a null)
- [ ] Your planned experiment/analysis can distinguish between hypotheses
- [ ] Wording is precise, neutral, and concise
- [ ] You can state the core hypothesis in 1–2 presentation-ready sentences
